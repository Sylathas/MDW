Anisotropy is a physical property meaning that some material properties are dependent on direction. An example of an anisotropic material is wood, thanks to its veneers that warp and distort based on humidity and dryness. A model may be created using modules, which ease both the construction and realization; then anisotropy governs and shapes the modules, generating a structure that, in the case of wood, reacts to humidity and moisture distribution in a space. These deformations could fulfill aesthetic functions, but can have practical uses too.




When talking about sustainability we always tend to think about recycling, but what about reusing? When we look at it from an ecological perspective, to reuse something brings the same benefits as recycling it, not only can enormous waste of material be avoided but on a design perspective we could start to look at product in a totally different way.

Nowadays this kind of transformation is difficult to envision inside products, but when we look at the future, which thinks and looks at the problems related to an eco-sustainable present to face a healthy future, this type of material can be fully exploited in various fields of industrial production, from objects to structures.

In a world like interior design new materials equal new possibilities, we tend to think about homes and cities as hard and solid, like a shelter from nature, but in a society that fully conquered nature what would it feel to soften up, for example by designing spaces able to adapt to the change of climate?

Think of a home interior whose spatial divisions are made by warm and welcoming mobile yet rigid, semi-sheer curtains. Rigid but flexible sheets of paper, mobile and resistant wood fabrics. Wood in the daily home environment could take on multiple ways of use, from a single container product to flexible movable walls. Wood has not only a tactility but also a certain scent that creates a physical presence and that transmits specific sensations related to the home and the sense of welcome.
